The Heat Equation¶

Statement of the Problem¶

Solve the following equation on a line (1D), a rectangle (2D) or a block (3D):

(1)¶\[\frac{\partial}{\partial t}T(\mathbf{x},t)-\mathcal{L}_{\mathbf{x}}[T(\mathbf{x},t)] = \Phi(\mathbf{x},t),\qquad \textrm{for }\mathbf{x}\in \Omega\]

with boundary conditions

(2)¶\[\mathcal{G}_{\mathbf{x}}[T(\mathbf{x},t)] = g(\mathbf{x},t),\qquad \textrm{for }\mathbf{x}\in \Gamma\]

and initial temperature distribution

(3)¶\[T(\mathbf{x},0)=T_0(\mathbf{x}),\qquad \textrm{at }t=0.\]

Where \(\Phi\) is an heat source (K/s), and where the operator \(\mathcal{L}_{\mathbf{x}}[T(\mathbf{x},t)]\) is the linear second order differential operator defined by:

\[\mathcal{L}_{\mathbf{x}}[T(\mathbf{x},t)] = \alpha \nabla^2 T(\mathbf{x},t)\]

The coefficient \(\alpha\), here considered constant, is the material diffusivity defined as:

\[\alpha = \frac{\kappa}{\rho C_p}\]

where \(\kappa\) is the thermal conductivity, \(\rho\) is the density, and \(C_p\) is the specific heat capacity.

Note

We consider here \(\alpha\) a constant larger than zero.

The operator \(\mathcal{G}^{(i)}_{\mathbf{x}}[T(\mathbf{x},t)]\) is a linear first order differential operator defined at the \(i^\textrm{th}\) boundary in \(\Gamma\).

(4)¶\[\mathcal{G}^{(i)}_{\mathbf{x}}[T(\mathbf{x},t)] = s_i\frac{\partial}{\partial \mathbf{\hat{n}}}\varphi(\mathbf{x}) + k_i\varphi(\mathbf{x}),\qquad \textrm{for }\mathbf{x}\in \Gamma^{(i)}\]

Where \(\frac{\partial}{\partial \mathbf{\hat{n}}}\) is the (outward) normal derivative at the boundary.

The operator \(\mathcal{G}^{(i)}_{\mathbf{x}}[T(\mathbf{x},t)]\) can be expressed differently depending on the type of boundary condition. These are classified into three main categories:

1- Temperature (Dirichlet): The function \(T(\mathbf{x},t)\) takes prescribed values at the boundary of the domain, \(s_i=0\).

2- Heat Flux (Neumann): The derivative along the (outward) normal is prescribed at the boundary of the domain, \(k_i=0\).

3- Convection (Robin): A linear relationship between the unknown function and its normal derivative is prescribed at the boundary of the domain, \(s_i\neq 0\) and \(k_i\neq 0\).

Overview of the method¶

The heat equation can be solved using Green’s Functions. The use of these functions allows defining a solution by superposition:

\[T(\mathbf{x}, t) = T_i(\mathbf{x}, t)+T_s(\mathbf{x}, t)+T_b^{(i)}(\mathbf{x}, t)\]

Where \(T_i(\mathbf{x}, t)\) is the initial term, \(T_s(\mathbf{x}, t)\) is the source term, and \(T_b^{(i)}(\mathbf{x}, t)\) is the \(i^\textrm{th}\) boundary term.

The Green’s function can be represented in the product form:

\[G(x,y,z,\xi,\eta,\zeta,t) = G_1(x,\xi,t)G_2(y,\eta,t)G_3(z,\zeta,t)\]

where \(G_1(x,\xi,t)\), \(G_2(y,\eta,t)\), and \(G_3(z,\zeta,t)\) are the Green’s functions of the one-dimensional boundary value problem defined along the \(x\)-, \(y\)-, and \(z\)-axis.

The idea is to multiply the one-dimensional solutions for each temperature contributions and simply add these contributions to obtain the solution.

The Homogeneous Solution in 1D¶

Using the method of separation of variable we can find a solution to the 3D homogeneous problem using a product of functions solution of the homogeneous 1D problem. If we choose \(x\) as the 1D spatial coordinate, the 1D homogeneous problem can be written as:

(5)¶\[\frac{\partial}{\partial t}T(x,t)-\mathcal{L}_{x}[T(x,t)] = 0\]

with homogeneous boundary conditions

(6)¶\[\mathcal{G}_{x}[T(x,t)] = 0,\qquad \textrm{for }x\in \Gamma\]

and initial condition

(7)¶\[T(x,0)=T_0(x),\qquad \textrm{at }t=0.\]

Substituting \(T(x,t) = \varphi(x)\psi(t)\) in equation (5) we obtain the following equations

(8)¶\[\frac{\partial^2 \varphi(x)}{\partial x^2} + \lambda \varphi(x) = 0\]
(9)¶\[\frac{\partial \psi(t)}{\partial t} + \lambda \alpha\psi(t) = 0\]

where \(\lambda\) is the separation variable.

The eigenfunctions solutions of equation (9) are obtained by direct integration:

(10)¶\[\psi_n(t) = C_n\exp(-\lambda_n\alpha t)\]

The spatial part of equations (5) to (7) can be expresssed as a Sturm-Liouville boundary problem, i.e.

(11)¶\[\mathcal{L}_{sl}[\varphi(x)]+\lambda\rho(x)\varphi(x)=0,\qquad \textrm{for }x\in [x_1,x_2]\]

with general homogeneous boundary conditions defined as

(12)¶\[s_1\frac{d \varphi(x)}{dx} + k_1\varphi(x) = 0,\qquad \textrm{at }x=x_1\]
(13)¶\[s_2\frac{d \varphi(x)}{dx} + k_2\varphi(x) = 0,\qquad \textrm{at }x=x_2\]

and initial condition

(14)¶\[\varphi_0(x) = T(x,0)\]

where

\[\mathcal{L}_{sl}[\varphi(x)]=\frac{d }{d x} \big(p(x)\frac{d\varphi(x)}{dx}\big)+q(x)\varphi(x)\]

With \(p(x)=1\), \(q(x)=0\), and \(\rho(x)=1\), and where the \(s_i\) and \(k_i\) are constants that depends on the type of boundary conditions – see Table 1.

From the Sturm-Liouville theory, it can be demonstrated that the eigenvalues \(\lambda_n\) of the homogeneous problem are real and positive. For \(\lambda_n>0\), the eigenfunctions of equation (8) have the form:

(15)¶\[\varphi_n(x) = A_n\cos(\sqrt{\lambda_n}x) + B_n\sin(\sqrt{\lambda_n}x)\]

The Sturm-Liouville theory shows that, in general, there is an infinite set of eigenvalues \(\lambda_n\) satisfying the given equation and the associated boundary conditions, and that these eigenvalues increase to infinity.

Corresponding to these eigenvalues, there exist an infinite set of orthogonal eigenfunctions \(\{\varphi_n(x)\}\) so that the linear superposition principle can be applied to find the convergent infinite series solution of the given problem. Formally, the regular Sturm-Liouville system can be expanded in an absolutely and uniformly convergent series:

(16)¶\[\varphi(x) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n\varphi_n(x)\]

where the coefficients \(a_n\) are defined using the properties of the regular Sturm-Liouville operator, i.e.

  1. The eigenfunctions of the adjoint problem have the same eigenvalues as the original problem.
  2. Eigenfunctions corresponding to different eigenvalues are orthogonal.

These properties giving:

The coefficients \(a_n\) are obtained using the inititial temperature distribution. If we substitute the initial condition \(\varphi_0(x)=T(x,0)\) in equation (16), we get

(17)¶\[\varphi_0(x) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n\varphi_n(x)\]

If we multiply both side of equation (17) by \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty \varphi_n(x)\) we obtain

\[\sum_{n=1}^\infty \varphi_n(x)\varphi_0(x) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \varphi_n(x)\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n \varphi_n(x)\]

then, knowing that:

\[\begin{split}\int_{x_1}^{x_2} \varphi_n(x)\varphi_m(x)dx = \left\{ \begin{array}{rl} 1 & \text{if } m = n,\\ 0 & \text{if } m\neq n. \end{array} \right.\end{split}\]

and integrating on the interval \([x_1,x_2]\)

\[\begin{split}\begin{eqnarray} \int_{x_1}^{x_2}\sum_{n=1}^\infty \varphi_n(\xi)\varphi_0(\xi)d\xi & = & \int_{x_1}^{x_2}\sum_{n=1}^\infty \varphi_n(\xi)\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n \varphi_n(\xi)d\xi\\ \sum_{n=1}^\infty \int_{x_1}^{x_2} \varphi_n(\xi)\varphi_0(\xi)d\xi & = & \sum_{n=1}^\infty \int_{x_1}^{x_2} a_n \varphi_n^2(\xi)d\xi \end{eqnarray}\end{split}\]

the coefficients \(a_n\) become

(18)¶\[a_n = \frac{1}{||\varphi_n||^2}\int_{x_1}^{x_2} \varphi_n(\xi)\varphi_0(\xi)d\xi\]

where

\[||\varphi_n||^2 = \int_{x_1}^{x_2} \varphi_n^2(\xi)d\xi\]

where \(\xi\) is a free parameter (integration variable).

Green’s Functions¶

From separation of variable, the homogeneous solution is:

(19)¶\[T(x,t) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n\varphi_n(x)\psi_n(t)\]

where the coefficients \(C_n\) are set to unity such that \(\psi_n(0)=1\) and the coefficients \(a_n\) are given by (18).

The Green’s function \(G(x,\mathbf{\xi},t)\) satisfy the homogeneous equation

(20)¶\[\frac{\partial}{\partial t}G - \mathcal{L}_{x}[G] = 0\]

with the nonhomogeneous initial condition (Dirac distribution)

(21)¶\[G = \delta(x,\xi), \qquad \textrm{at }t=\tau\]

and homogeneous boundary conditions

(22)¶\[s_1\frac{\partial }{\partial x}G + k_1 G = 0, \qquad \textrm{at }x=x_1\]
(23)¶\[s_2\frac{\partial }{\partial x}G + k_2 G = 0, \qquad \textrm{at }x=x_2\]

At \(t=0\), \(\psi_n=1\), and \(G=\delta(x,\xi)\), which means that:

(24)¶\[T(x,0) = \int_{x_1}^{x_2}G\varphi_0(\xi)d\xi = \varphi_0(x)\]

using (19) we have

(25)¶\[T(x,0) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n\varphi_n(x) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{\varphi_n(x)}{||\varphi_n||^2}\int_{x_1}^{x_2} \varphi_n(\xi)\varphi_0(\xi)d\xi\]

Comparing (24) and (25) we can see that

\[G(x,\xi,0) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{\varphi_n(\xi)\varphi_n(x)}{||\varphi_n||^2}\]

Using (20) we can see that more generally

\[G(x,\xi,t) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{\varphi_n(\xi)\varphi_n(x)}{||\varphi_n||^2}\psi_n(t)\]

Superposition¶

The final solution is obtained by splitting the problem in three, i.e.

\[T(x,t) = T_i(x, t)+T_s(x, t)+T_b(x, t)\]

Initial Contribution

The initial part \(T_i(x,t)\) is defined by

(26)¶\[\frac{\partial}{\partial t}T_i(x,t)-\mathcal{L}_{x}[T_i(x,t)] = 0,\qquad \textrm{for }x\in \Omega\]

with boundary conditions

(27)¶\[\mathcal{G}_{x}[T_i(x,t)] = 0,\qquad \textrm{for }x\in \Gamma\]

and nonhomogeneous initial temperature distribution

(28)¶\[T_i(x,0)=T_0(x),\qquad \textrm{at }t=0.\]

With

\[T_0(x) = \lim_{t\rightarrow \tau}\int_{x_1}^{x_2}T_0(\xi)G(x,\xi,t-\tau)d\xi\]

we get

\[T_i(x,t) = \int_{x_1}^{x_2}G(x,\xi,t)T_0(\xi)d\xi\]

Source Contribution

The source part \(T_s(x,t)\) is defined by

(29)¶\[\frac{\partial}{\partial t}T_s(x,t)-\mathcal{L}_{x}[T_s(x,t)] = \Phi(x,t),\qquad \textrm{for }x\in \Omega\]

with boundary conditions

(30)¶\[\mathcal{G}_{x}[T_s(x,t)] = 0,\qquad \textrm{for }x\in \Gamma\]

and initial temperature distribution

(31)¶\[T_s(x,0)=0,\qquad \textrm{at }t=0.\]

With (8) and \(T_{s,n}(x,t)=\varphi_n(x)\psi_n(t)\) we have

(32)¶\[\sum_{n=1}^\infty\frac{\partial }{\partial t}T_{s,n}(x,t) +\alpha\lambda_n T_{s,n}(x,t) = \Phi(x,t)\]

Multiplying both side of (32) by \(\exp(\alpha\lambda_n t)\) we get

(33)¶\[\sum_{n=1}^\infty\frac{\partial }{\partial t}T_{s,n}(x,t)\exp(\alpha\lambda_n t) +\alpha\lambda_n T_{s,n}(x,t)\exp(\alpha\lambda_n t) = \Phi(x,t)\sum_{n=1}^\infty\exp(\alpha\lambda_n t)\]

which is equivalent to:

(34)¶\[\sum_{n=1}^\infty\frac{\partial }{\partial t}\left(T_{s,n}(x,t)\exp(\alpha\lambda_n t)\right) = \Phi(x,t)\sum_{n=1}^\infty\exp(\alpha\lambda_n t)\]

Integrating on both side of (34) gives

(35)¶\[\sum_{n=1}^\infty T_{s,n}(x,t)\exp(\alpha\lambda_n t) = \int_0^t\Phi(x,\tau)\sum_{n=1}^\infty\exp(\alpha\lambda_n \tau)d\tau\]

or

(36)¶\[T_{s}(x,t) = \int_0^t\Phi(x,\tau)\sum_{n=1}^\infty\exp(-\alpha\lambda_n (t-\tau))d\tau\]

At \(t=\tau\) \(G=\delta(x,\xi)\) and

\[\Phi(x,\tau)=\int_{x_1}^{x_2} G(x,\xi,0)\Phi(x,\tau)d\xi\]

which gives

\[T_s(x,t) = \int_0^t G(x,\xi,0)\Phi(x,\tau)\sum_{n=1}^\infty\exp(-\alpha\lambda_n (t-\tau))d\xi d\tau\]

or

\[T_s(x,t) = \int_0^t G(x,\xi,t-\tau)\Phi(x,\tau)d\xi d\tau\]

Boundary Contribution

The boundary part \(T_b(x,t)\) is defined by

(37)¶\[\frac{\partial}{\partial t}T_b(x,t)-\mathcal{L}_{x}[T_b(x,t)] = 0,\qquad \textrm{for }x\in \Omega\]

with boundary conditions

(38)¶\[s_1\frac{\partial }{\partial x}T_b(x,t) + k_1 T_b(x,t) = g_1, \qquad \textrm{at }x=x_1\]
(39)¶\[s_2\frac{\partial }{\partial x}T_b(x,t) + k_2 T_b(x,t) = g_2, \qquad \textrm{at }x=x_2\]

and initial temperature distribution

(40)¶\[T_b(x,0)=0,\qquad \textrm{at }t=0.\]

From (20) we have

(41)¶\[\int_{x_1}^{x_2}T_b\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial \tau}G-\alpha\frac{\partial^2}{\partial \xi^2}G\right)d\xi = 0\]

From the Green’s second identity we can see that

(42)¶\[\int_{x_1}^{x_2}\left(T_b\frac{\partial^2}{\partial \xi^2}G - G\frac{\partial^2}{\partial \xi^2}T_b\right)d\xi =\left[T_b\frac{\partial}{\partial \xi}G - G\frac{\partial}{\partial \xi}T_b\right]_{\xi=x_1}^{\xi=x_2}\]

using (20) we have

(43)¶\[\frac{\partial^2}{\partial \xi^2}G = \frac{1}{\alpha}\frac{\partial}{\partial t}G\]

Substituting (43) in (42) we get

(44)¶\[\int_{x_1}^{x_2}\left(T_b\frac{1}{\alpha}\frac{\partial}{\partial t}G - G\frac{\partial^2}{\partial \xi^2}T_b\right)d\xi =\left[T_b\frac{\partial}{\partial \xi}G - G\frac{\partial}{\partial \xi}T_b\right]_{\xi=x_1}^{\xi=x_2}\]

or multiplying by \(\alpha\) on both side of (44)

(45)¶\[\int_{x_1}^{x_2}\left(T_b\frac{\partial}{\partial t}G - \alpha G\frac{\partial^2}{\partial \xi^2}T_b\right)d\xi =\alpha\left[T_b\frac{\partial}{\partial \xi}G - G\frac{\partial}{\partial \xi}T_b\right]_{\xi=x_1}^{\xi=x_2}\]

Using (37), and integrating both side of (45) over \(\tau\) gives

(46)¶\[\int_0^t\int_{x_1}^{x_2}\left(T_b\frac{\partial}{\partial \tau}G + G\frac{\partial}{\partial \tau}T_b\right)d\xi d\tau=\alpha\int_0^t\left[T_b\frac{\partial}{\partial \xi}G - G\frac{\partial}{\partial \xi}T_b\right]_{\xi=x_1}^{\xi=x_2}d\tau\]

Solution

Combining all these terms gives:

(47)¶\[\begin{split}T(x,t) = \int_{x_1}^{x_2} T_0(\xi)G(x,\xi,t)d\xi +\\ \int_0^t \int_{x_1}^{x_2} \Phi(\xi,t-\tau)G(x,\xi,t-\tau) d\xi d\tau +\\ \alpha\int_0^t g_1(\xi,t-\tau)\Lambda_1(x,\xi,t,\tau) d\tau +\\ \alpha\int_0^t g_2(\xi,t-\tau)\Lambda_2(x,\xi,t,\tau) d\tau\end{split}\]

Where \(\Lambda_i\) is a function defined by the boundary conditions.

Table 2: \(\Lambda_i\) for Different Homogeneous Boundary Type.¶
Boundary Type \(k_1\) \(k_2\) \(s_1\) \(s_2\) \(\Lambda_1,~x=x_1\) \(\Lambda_2,~x=x_2\)
Temperature, Dirichlet 1 1 0 0 \(\frac{\partial}{\partial \xi}G(x,\xi,t-\tau)\) \(-\frac{\partial}{\partial \xi}G(x,\xi,t-\tau)\)
Heat Flux, Neumann 0 0 1 1 \(-G(x,\xi,t-\tau)\) \(G(x,\xi,t-\tau)\)
Convection, Robin <0 >0 1 1 \(-G(x,\xi,t-\tau)\) \(G(x,\xi,t-\tau)\)
Mixed I 1 0 0 1 \(\frac{\partial}{\partial \xi}G(x,\xi,t-\tau)\) \(G(x,\xi,t-\tau)\)
Mixed II 0 1 1 0 \(-G(x,\xi,t-\tau)\) \(-\frac{\partial}{\partial \xi}G(x,\xi,t-\tau)\)
